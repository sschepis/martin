# Scene Design System

## 1. Start with the dramatic function

Before choosing shots, define what the scene is doing.

Ask:

* Who has power at the start?
* Who has power at the end?
* Is the scene about revelation, tension, intimacy, confusion, wonder, distance, or momentum?
* Should the viewer feel stable, trapped, invited, overwhelmed, or uncertain?
* Is the scene externally active or internally active?

That gives you the visual logic.

---

## 2. Core mapping: intention to shot language

## A. To build tension

**Visual pattern**

* Begin wider and more stable
* Gradually reduce frame size
* Add selective movement toward subject
* Increase cutting frequency only if needed

**Useful choices**

* Medium shots to establish interaction
* Medium close-ups as stakes sharpen
* Close-ups when emotional compression begins
* Slow dolly-in or subtle push-in
* Shallow depth of field
* Low-key lighting
* Occasional insert shots on hands, objects, eyes

**What it evokes**

* Compression
* Escalation
* Psychological narrowing
* Imminence

**Typical sequence**

1. Wide or medium wide to establish spatial relation
2. Medium two-shot for interaction
3. OTS coverage
4. Medium close-up on reaction
5. Push-in close-up at critical line
6. Insert on decisive detail

---

## B. To create intimacy

**Visual pattern**

* Favor proximity over spectacle
* Keep movement restrained and gentle
* Let faces hold the frame longer

**Useful choices**

* Medium close-up
* Close-up
* Eye-level angle
* Soft, steady camera
* Shallow depth of field
* Longer takes
* Naturalistic or practical lighting

**What it evokes**

* Vulnerability
* Emotional access
* Trust
* Human presence

**Typical sequence**

1. Medium shot to orient
2. Medium close-up for mutual attention
3. Close-up for emotional disclosure
4. Hold on reaction instead of cutting away

---

## C. To show dominance or threat

**Visual pattern**

* Use angle, framing, and spatial control
* Make the subject feel structurally larger or more stable than others

**Useful choices**

* Low angle on dominant character
* Wider lens close to subject for imposing presence
* Static or slow deliberate movement
* Centered composition
* Backlighting or strong contrast
* Wider frames showing others arranged around them

**What it evokes**

* Authority
* Fear
* Weight
* Pressure

**Typical sequence**

1. Eye-level group setup
2. Low-angle medium shot on dominant figure
3. High-angle reaction on weaker figure
4. Slow push-in as control tightens

---

## D. To show vulnerability or defeat

**Visual pattern**

* Reduce subject’s visual control
* Open more empty space around them or look down on them

**Useful choices**

* High angle
* Wide shot with isolation
* Negative space
* Pull-back movement
* Longer lens for emotional flattening
* Dim or directional light

**What it evokes**

* Exposure
* Helplessness
* Loneliness
* Emotional diminishment

**Typical sequence**

1. Medium shot during active exchange
2. High-angle medium close-up after loss
3. Wide pull-back to show isolation

---

## E. To create unease or disorientation

**Visual pattern**

* Break visual stability
* Disturb horizon, rhythm, or continuity

**Useful choices**

* Dutch angle
* Handheld
* Whip pans
* Jump cuts
* Extreme close-ups
* Wide-angle distortion
* Fragmented inserts
* Uneven or flickering light

**What it evokes**

* Instability
* Anxiety
* Mental fracture
* Loss of orientation

**Typical sequence**

1. Start relatively normal
2. Introduce slight instability in framing
3. Move to tighter distorted coverage
4. Add abrupt edits or erratic movement

---

## F. To evoke wonder, awe, or transcendence

**Visual pattern**

* Expand scale
* Reveal space gradually or majestically
* Use motion that feels lifted or fluid

**Useful choices**

* Extreme wide shot
* Crane or jib
* Slow tilt up
* Deep focus
* Backlight, atmosphere, silhouette
* Long take
* Symmetrical or expansive composition

**What it evokes**

* Vastness
* Reverence
* Discovery
* Enlargement of perspective

**Typical sequence**

1. Restrained close framing
2. Reveal through tilt, dolly, or crane
3. Full environmental shot
4. Hold to let scale register

---

## G. To communicate speed, pursuit, or urgency

**Visual pattern**

* Favor directional movement
* Keep compositions dynamic
* Maintain clear geography unless chaos is intended

**Useful choices**

* Tracking shots
* Handheld or steadicam
* Trucking movement
* Wider lenses
* Shorter shot duration
* Inserts for critical action beats

**What it evokes**

* Momentum
* Pressure
* Immediate danger
* Embodied movement

**Typical sequence**

1. Wide to define route
2. Tracking profile or front-facing run
3. Inserts on feet, door handles, weapon, phone
4. Cross-cuts if parallel action matters

---

## H. To show revelation or realization

**Visual pattern**

* Shift from external observation to internal recognition
* Let attention narrow at the moment of discovery

**Useful choices**

* Rack focus
* Slow push-in
* Insert shot
* Close-up reaction
* Sudden stillness after motion
* Slight sound or visual simplification

**What it evokes**

* Recognition
* Cognitive shift
* Meaning landing
* Sudden coherence

**Typical sequence**

1. Medium shot with scene activity
2. Insert or focus shift to key detail
3. Close-up reaction
4. Hold for processing

---

## 3. Simple recipe builder

Use this formula:

**Scene intent** + **power structure** + **subjectivity level** + **energy level** = **shot plan**

Example:

**Intent:** tension
**Power structure:** shifting
**Subjectivity:** medium-high
**Energy:** restrained
**Result:** start with stable medium two-shots, move into tighter OTS coverage, introduce subtle push-ins, end on close-up with shallow depth of field.

---

## 4. Shot progression templates

## Template 1: Dialogue tension escalation

Use when two characters are verbally circling conflict.

1. Wide two-shot

   * Establish relation and distance

2. Medium two-shot

   * Narrow attention to interaction

3. OTS on speaker A

   * Align viewer with one side

4. OTS on speaker B

   * Counterpoint and rhythm

5. Medium close-up on A

   * Stakes sharpen

6. Medium close-up on B

   * Emotional detail becomes visible

7. Close-up on decisive line

   * Compression and impact

8. Reaction close-up

   * Consequence lands

---

## Template 2: Intimate confession

1. Medium shot
2. Medium close-up
3. Close-up on confessor
4. Hold on listener reaction
5. Return to shared frame only after emotional shift

This works because intimacy usually strengthens when the frame narrows and cutting slows.

---

## Template 3: Threat entrance

1. Establishing shot of environment
2. Character notices something off-screen
3. Slow pan or rack focus reveal
4. Low-angle medium shot of threatening figure
5. High-angle or tight reaction on observer
6. Hold rather than cut rapidly

This lets threat arrive with shape and weight.

---

## Template 4: Realization beat

1. Medium shot with ongoing action
2. Insert of clue
3. Rack focus or push-in
4. Close-up reaction
5. Brief pause before next action

The pause is often what makes the realization feel real.

---

## Template 5: Isolation after loss

1. Close-up during emotional event
2. Silence or reduced motion
3. Pull back to medium
4. Pull back to wide with negative space
5. Hold

This turns private pain into spatial fact.

---

## 5. Emotional control through distance

A useful rule:

* **Wide** = relationship to world
* **Medium** = relationship to others
* **Close** = relationship to self
* **Extreme close** = relationship to a single thought, sensation, or symbol

So when designing a scene, choose whether the viewer should feel the world, the interaction, the person, or the detail.

---

## 6. Emotional control through movement

Another useful rule:

* **Static** = observation, restraint, certainty
* **Slow push-in** = attention intensifying
* **Pull-back** = emotional distancing or contextualization
* **Lateral tracking** = continuity and movement through process
* **Handheld** = embodied instability
* **Crane** = release, scale, elevation

---

## 7. Emotional control through angle

* **Eye level** = equal regard
* **Low angle** = force, control, stature
* **High angle** = exposure, reduction
* **Overhead** = abstraction, destiny, system-view
* **Dutch angle** = inner or outer imbalance

---

## 8. A fast decision matrix

If you want the viewer to feel:

**Close to a character**

* medium close-up / close-up
* eye-level
* shallow depth of field
* steady camera

**Unsafe**

* handheld
* tighter framing
* low-key light
* Dutch angle or obstructed composition

**A power imbalance**

* low angle on one subject
* high angle on the other
* asymmetrical framing
* different lensing or distance

**Wonder**

* wide or extreme wide
* deep focus
* upward reveal
* long take

**Mental fragmentation**

* jump cuts
* extreme close-ups
* unstable horizon
* abrupt lens or framing changes

## 9. One practical scene generator

You can use this fill-in structure for any scene:

**Scene goal:**
What changes by the end?

**Primary emotion:**
Tension, grief, intimacy, awe, confusion, dread, triumph

**Power at start:**
Who controls the scene?

**Power at end:**
Who controls the scene?

**Viewer alignment:**
Whose inner state should dominate?

**Visual progression:**
Wide → Medium → Close?
Static → Moving?
Level → Tilted?
Shared frame → Isolated singles?

**Climax shot:**
What is the one shot that carries the scene’s decisive meaning?

## 10. Worked examples

## Example: “Build tension between two characters at a dinner table”

**Goal:** polite conversation becomes threat
**Primary emotion:** restrained tension
**Power shift:** balanced to one-sided
**Viewer alignment:** alternating, then favor the vulnerable one

**Shot sequence**

1. Wide two-shot across table
2. Medium singles in OTS pattern
3. Insert of hand tightening on glass
4. Medium close-up on aggressor, very steady
5. Medium close-up on vulnerable character, shallower focus
6. Slow push-in on final accusation
7. Silent reaction close-up
8. Wide shot with new emotional distance

**Why it works**
The progression reduces social space and increases psychological pressure.

---

## Example: “A character discovers a beautiful hidden valley”

**Goal:** transition from uncertainty to awe
**Primary emotion:** wonder
**Power shift:** from human-scale effort to environmental vastness
**Viewer alignment:** explorer

**Shot sequence**

1. Tight medium following character through brush
2. Over-the-shoulder partial reveal
3. Slow step forward with steadicam or dolly
4. Tilt up or crane reveal into extreme wide
5. Hold on landscape
6. Close-up reaction with soft light
7. Return to wide for scale confirmation

**Why it works**
You delay the full reveal so the expansion of the frame becomes the emotional event.

---

## Example: “A person realizes they have been betrayed”

**Goal:** information turns into emotional rupture
**Primary emotion:** shock into hurt
**Power shift:** perceived safety to collapse
**Viewer alignment:** betrayed character

**Shot sequence**

1. Medium conversation shot
2. Insert on message / object / expression
3. Rack focus or cut to detail
4. Close-up on eyes processing
5. Sound and movement simplify
6. Slow pull-back as emotional distance opens
7. Wide isolated frame

**Why it works**
Revelation lands in a close frame, then expands into emptiness.

---

## 11. Condensed master rule

A scene usually feels strongest when the visual grammar changes with the dramatic state.

If the scene escalates, the shots should usually compress, intensify, isolate, or destabilize.

If the scene opens into freedom, truth, awe, or release, the shots should usually widen, lift, steady, or breathe.
